Unmasking the Government Grant Scam: Protecting Older Adults from Financial Loss and Identity Theft

Published 2026-03-15 · Is This a Scam? · isthisascam.news

Scammers have devised a sophisticated scheme to steal personal information and money by posing as government officials offering free grants. Victims are often older adults who trust authority figures, leading to significant financial loss and identity theft.

How It Works

The scam typically starts with a call from someone claiming to be from an official agency such as the IRS or Social Security Administration. The caller says you’ve qualified for a new government benefit program but must act quickly to claim it. They often spoof legitimate government numbers on caller ID, making their claims seem more credible.

The script might go like this: “Hello, Mr./Ms. [Victim’s Name], we’re calling from the Department of Social Security. You’ve qualified for a special grant worth $5000 to help with your monthly expenses. To claim it, we just need some verification information.” They’ll ask for personal details such as your Social Security number or bank account information.

Why It Works on Older Adults

Older adults are particularly vulnerable because they often have fixed incomes and may be more trusting of authority figures. Scammers exploit this trust by posing as government officials, creating a sense of urgency that can lead to hasty decisions. Additionally, the promise of free money is an attractive lure for those on limited budgets.

Real Numbers

The FTC reports that these scams have cost victims millions in losses and identity theft cases. In fact, $47.2 million has been returned to consumers deceived by similar fraudulent schemes involving undisclosed fees from companies like Invitation Homes.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • You’ve won a grant but need to pay upfront fees.
  • Your personal information is needed for verification right away.
  • The caller insists on immediate action, creating urgency and confusion.
  • The call comes from a spoofed number that appears legitimate on caller ID.

What to Do Right Now

1. Hang up immediately if you receive such a call or email.

2. Contact your parent and have an open conversation about government grant scams using the script below:

“Mom/Dad, I read about a new scam where scammers pose as government officials offering free grants. They ask for personal information like Social Security numbers to verify eligibility. Can you please let me know if anyone has contacted you recently? If they do call or email, don’t give them any information and hang up.”

3. Set up a fraud alert with the major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) to monitor your parent’s credit.

Report It

If you or someone you know has been targeted by this scam, report it immediately to the Federal Trade Commission at 1-877-FTC-HELP (1-877-382-4357) and file a complaint with the FTC’s Complaint Assistant online. You can also contact your local law enforcement agency for further assistance.
