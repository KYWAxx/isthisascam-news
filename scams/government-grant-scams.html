Protecting Older Adults From Government Grant Scams

Published 2026-03-15 · Is This a Scam? · isthisascam.news

Scammers have devised a new tactic, claiming that victims are eligible for free money through fabricated government programs. This scam can lead to significant financial losses or identity theft when older adults share personal information.

How It Works

The call starts with someone identifying as an official from a recognized agency like the Social Security Administration or Medicare. They’ll inform you that your parent is eligible for a new government grant and must act quickly to claim it. The script often goes something like this:

'Hello, this is [Name] from the Department of Health and Human Services. We have a special benefit program for which you are qualified. To get started, we just need some verification details.'

Why It Works on Older Adults

Older adults often trust authority figures more easily and may be less familiar with how government programs actually operate. The urgency created by the caller—often demanding immediate action to claim a benefit—is designed to confuse and pressure victims into sharing sensitive information.

Real Numbers

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reports that $47.2 million has been returned to consumers deceived by similar scams, including fake government grants. The FTC estimates that these scams have affected thousands of older adults nationwide.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • You’ve won a grant but need to pay upfront fees.
  • Your personal information is needed for verification right away.
  • The caller insists you act fast or miss out on the opportunity.

What to Do Right Now

1. Hang up immediately if anyone calls claiming you’ve won a government grant and requires your personal details.

2. Contact your parent’s bank and set up alerts for any unusual activity or withdrawals.

3. Have a conversation with your parent using this script:

'Mom/Dad, I read about a new scam where scammers claim to be from the government offering grants but ask for personal information. If someone calls claiming you’re eligible for a grant and asks for anything like your Social Security number or bank details, hang up right away. Let’s talk if you get any such calls.'

Report It

If you suspect a scam or identity theft, report it to the Federal Trade Commission at 1-877-FTC-HELP (382-4357) or file a complaint online. You can also contact your local police department for assistance.'
