Unveiling the Invitation Homes Rental Fee Fraud: How Scammers Exploit Trust and Conceal Costs

Published 2026-03-15 · Is This a Scam? · isthisascam.news

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has sent checks totaling more than $47.2 million to consumers deceived by Invitation Homes, a significant landlord of single-family homes. Older adults, often relying on stable rental agreements without hidden costs, have been particularly vulnerable to this scheme.

How It Works

The fraud operates through a deceptive practice where Invitation Homes included undisclosed fees and unlawful charges within rental contracts. Victims were unaware they were overpaying for extended periods due to these hidden costs. Here’s how the scam unfolds:

  • A potential renter inquires about an available property.
  • Invitation Homes presents a contract that appears straightforward, but it includes undisclosed fees and charges buried within fine print.
  • Renters sign the contracts without realizing they are agreeing to additional costs beyond the advertised price.

The scam continues with renters paying these hidden fees for months or even years before becoming aware of the deception. The FTC has issued refunds, but many older adults may still be unaware of the issue and continue to overpay.

Why It Works on Older Adults

Older adults are particularly susceptible due to their tendency to trust legitimate companies and rely on stable rental agreements. Scammers exploit this trust by concealing fees within contracts, making it difficult for victims to identify the deception. Additionally, many older adults may not have experience navigating complex legal documents or recognizing fraudulent practices.

Real Numbers

The FTC has issued checks totaling more than $47.2 million to eligible consumers deceived by Invitation Homes. This figure represents the financial impact of undisclosed fees and unlawful charges on renters between January 2021 and September 2024, affecting thousands of victims.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • 'Congratulations! We’ve found additional funds for your expenses.' — a common red flag in government grant scams.
  • 'We are from Microsoft tech support. Your PC is infected; we can fix it.' — indicative of tech support scams.
  • 'I need these gift cards urgently for business purposes.' — a tactic used in gift card scams.

What to Do Right Now

To protect your aging parents from this fraud, take the following steps:

  • Contact them and review their rental contracts carefully. Look for any hidden fees or charges.
  • Encourage them to reach out to Invitation Homes directly if they suspect undisclosed costs.
  • Discuss recent FTC refunds and ensure they have received any checks due to them.

Here’s a script you can use when talking to your parent:

“Hi Mom/Dad, I read about some issues with hidden fees in rental contracts. Can we go over yours together? It might be worth checking if there are any extra costs that weren’t obvious at first.”

Report It

If you or your parent has fallen victim to this fraud or any other scam, report it immediately:

  • Contact the FTC at 1-877-FTC-HELP (1-877-382-4357).
  • File a complaint with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov.
