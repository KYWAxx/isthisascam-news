The Prize Notification Fraud: Unraveling the Scheme Behind Fake Winnings

Published 2026-03-15 · Is This a Scam? · isthisascam.news

Scammers are using a simple yet effective tactic to rob older adults of millions: fake prize notifications that appear to be from legitimate companies like 'ABC Sweepstakes.' The con often starts with an urgent phone call or text, followed by a request for money through gift cards or wire transfers. Last year alone, over $30 million was lost to these schemes.

How It Works

The scam begins when you receive an unexpected phone call or text message displaying a legitimate-sounding company's name like 'ABC Sweepstakes' on caller ID. The script is designed for urgency: “Congratulations! You’ve won $10,000 in our holiday giveaway!” followed by instructions to send money via gift cards, wire transfers, or prepaid debit cards to cover taxes and shipping fees.

Why It Works on Older Adults

The combination of unexpected good news and a sense of urgency makes this scam particularly effective. Many older adults may feel excited about the prospect of winning a large sum of money and are more likely to comply with requests for personal information or payment, especially if they've been conditioned to trust institutions like sweepstakes companies.

Real Numbers

Last year alone, over $30 million was lost by individuals falling victim to prize scams according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3). These losses are spread across thousands of victims who believed they had won a substantial sum of money only to find themselves out of pocket.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • 'You’ve won $10,000 in our holiday giveaway!' — Scammers use urgency and big numbers to create excitement.
  • 'We need your bank details to process it.' — Legitimate prize notifications never require immediate payment or personal banking information.
  • 'Please buy these gift cards quickly to claim the prize.' — This is how scammers get their money, often requesting specific amounts that must be purchased immediately.
  • 'Your winnings are time-sensitive and require urgent action.' — Scammers create a sense of urgency to pressure victims into acting before they think it through.

What to Do Right Now

To protect your loved ones, take these concrete actions:

  • Talk to them about the risk. Use this script: “Mom/Dad, have you heard about prize scams? They’ll call and say you’ve won money but then ask for payment or personal information. It’s a scam.”
  • Set up two-factor authentication on all their accounts.
  • Create a list of trusted contacts they can verify the source with before sending any money.

Report It

If you suspect someone has fallen victim to this scam, report it immediately. Contact the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at www.ic3.gov, or call them directly at 1-800-CALL-FBI.
